Abstract — We study image classification in a low-data regime on Tiny ImageNet. Our central question is whether synthetic images from pretrained diffusion models can recover performance lost when only 5% of training data is available, and whether any recovery reflects genuine representation improvement. We introduce three pipelines: Baseline (5% real), DiffusionBoost (5% real + synthetic), and Ceiling (100% real). Stage 1 establishes a robust data pipeline, trains a ResNet-18 baseline, and produces accuracy, calibration, robustness, and representation diagnostics. The model achieves 37.15% Top-1 accuracy with ECE 0.0398. In Stage 2, we will extend to diffusion augmentation and investigate whether synthetic utility varies across classes. 
Keywords— Low-data classification, synthetic data augmentation, diffusion models, Tiny ImageNet, calibration (ECE), representation geometry, ResNet-18.
Problem Definition
We study image classification in a low-data regime on Tiny ImageNet (200 classes, 64×64 images). Our central question is whether synthetic images generated by large pretrained diffusion models can recover performance lost when only 5% of the real training data is available, and whether any apparent recovery reflects genuine representation improvement rather than trivial data smoothing.
Formally, the input is an image x∈R^(3×64×64) and the output is a class label y∈{1,…,200}. The low-data setting (5% of Tiny ImageNet, i.e., 25 images per class) makes the problem challenging due to overfitting, miscalibration, and brittleness to small corruptions. We introduce three pipelines: (1) a Baseline trained on 5% real images with classical augmentations, (2) a DiffusionBoost model that mixes real images with class-conditional synthetic samples from Stable Diffusion, and (3) a Ceiling model trained on the full Tiny ImageNet training set.
Stage 1 focuses on building a robust data pipeline, training a pretrained ResNet-18 backbone, and producing initial accuracy, calibration, robustness, and feature-geometry diagnostics. In Stage 2, we will extend this pipeline with diffusion-generated synthetic data and investigate whether the benefit of augmentation is uniform across classes — with implications for how synthetic data should be generated and allocated.
Mini Literature Survey
Learning in Low-data Regimes
Modern computer vision systems typically rely on large datasets, yet many practical settings operate under severe data scarcity. Recent research emphasizes developing methods that remain effective when labelled data is limited. Studies on data-efficient AI highlight that performance degradation in such regimes arises from overfitting, poor feature coverage, and unstable generalization when deep networks are trained on small datasets [1]. Consequently, recent work has explored generative approaches that artificially expand datasets to improve sample efficiency and representation learning.
Synthetic Data Generation and Diffusion-based Augmentation
Recent advances in generative models—particularly diffusion models—have enabled high-fidelity synthetic image generation that can be used for data augmentation. Diffusion-based augmentation methods generate class-conditional images that increase training diversity while preserving semantic structure. For example, recent work has demonstrated that synthetic images generated by diffusion models can improve classification robustness and generalisation in low-data settings by supplementing scarce real data [2]. Other work proposes structured diffusion-based augmentation strategies that guide generation toward class-informative samples, improving performance particularly in few-shot regimes [3]. Hybrid training strategies combining real and synthetic images have also been shown to improve representation learning and model robustness compared with training on real or synthetic data alone [4]. These studies suggest that diffusion models can produce useful training data, but the degree to which such synthetic samples provide genuine information gain remains an open question.
Synthetic Data Scaling and Augmentation Design
Recent work also examines how the quantity and structure of synthetic data affect downstream model performance. Empirical studies demonstrate that models trained with both real and synthetic samples can outperform those trained only on real data in certain conditions, though the benefit depends on generation quality and distributional alignment with real images [5]. Other research explores strategies such as context-guided filtering of generated images to remove low-quality samples before augmentation, highlighting that the usefulness of synthetic data depends not only on quantity but also on fidelity and semantic consistency [6]. These findings motivate systematic evaluations of synthetic augmentation under controlled experimental settings.
Synthetic Data for Improving Robustness and Generalisation
Beyond accuracy improvements, several recent studies investigate whether synthetic data can improve model robustness and generalization. Experiments across multiple visual recognition tasks show that models trained with a combination of synthetic and real data often achieve improved robustness to distribution shifts compared with models trained on limited real data alone [7]. These findings suggest that generative augmentation may enrich the diversity of training samples and improve feature coverage, though the relationship between synthetic diversity and representation learning remains under-explored.
Representation Similarity and Feature Geometry
Understanding how synthetic augmentation affects internal representations has become an active research direction. Recent representation analysis methods examine feature covariance spectra, linear probe separability, and representation similarity metrics to study how neural networks learn structured feature spaces. In particular, Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) has emerged as a widely used technique for comparing representations between neural networks trained under different regimes, enabling researchers to quantify whether training modifications shift representations toward more generalizable feature structures. Such analyses help determine whether improvements from synthetic augmentation reflect genuine representation learning rather than superficial regularization effects.
Positioning of the Present Study
Building on this literature, our work investigates diffusion-based synthetic augmentation under a controlled low-data classification setting using Tiny ImageNet. Unlike prior studies that focus primarily on accuracy improvements, we evaluate synthetic augmentation across multiple axes—accuracy recovery, calibration, corruption robustness, and representation geometry. This multi-axis analysis allows us to examine whether diffusion-generated images merely increase sample counts or whether they meaningfully alter the learned feature space toward the structure obtained with full real-data training.
Exploratory Data Analysis
We use Tiny ImageNet as a controlled yet realistic benchmark. The full dataset contains 200 classes with 500 training images and 50 validation images per class. To simulate a low-data regime, we construct a deterministic 5% stratified subset of the training split: for each class, we randomly sample 25 images (seeded), yielding exactly 5,000 training samples.
Our EDA confirms that the resulting subset is perfectly balanced by design (25 images per class), eliminating class imbalance as a confound. Figure 1 shows a grid of sample images spanning multiple classes to qualitatively inspect diversity and label quality.
 
Sample images from the 5% Tiny ImageNet subset across multiple classes
All images are originally 64×64 pixels; a simple size histogram verifies the uniform resolution. We also compute channel-wise means and standard deviations and find them to be broadly consistent with standard ImageNet statistics, justifying the use of ImageNet normalisation. For Stage 1, we resize inputs to 224×224 and apply standard augmentations (random crops, horizontal flips).
Baseline and Proof of Concept
Our baseline model is a ResNet-18 pretrained on ImageNet. We replace the final fully connected layer with a 200-way classifier to match Tiny ImageNet classes and fine-tune the model on the 5% subset using AdamW (learning rate 3×10⁻⁴, batch size 64) for 5 epochs. All experiments use mixed-precision training on a single RTX 4060 GPU. Figure 2 shows the training and validation loss/accuracy curves over epochs.
 
Training Loss and validation accuracy over 5 epochs for the ResNet-18 baseline
The model achieves 37.15% Top-1 accuracy on the Tiny ImageNet validation set after 5 epochs, which satisfies the Stage 1 requirement of obtaining a non-trivial baseline. Sample predictions on held-out images confirm the pipeline runs end-to-end.
Beyond accuracy, we compute Expected Calibration Error (ECE) on the validation set and obtain ECE = 0.0398. Figure 3 shows the reliability diagram, indicating the model is mildly over-confident in this low-data setting.
 
Reliability diagram (ECE = 0.0398) on the full validation set
We also perform a corruption benchmark (Gaussian noise, blur, brightness shift) and observe drops from 37.15% on clean data to 10.17%, 36.72%, and 31.15% under perturbations, quantifying robustness. Finally, we extract penultimate-layer features, compute the covariance eigenvalue spectrum, and train a linear probe, obtaining 42.00% accuracy — providing an initial snapshot of representation geometry for later comparison against diffusion-augmented models.
In Stage 2, we will introduce class-conditional synthetic images from Stable Diffusion and evaluate the augmented pipeline across calibration, robustness, and representation geometry using CKA similarity and feature covariance spectra. We will examine whether synthetic utility varies across semantic categories, and whether class-level properties from this baseline can predict which classes benefit. Experiments will be replicated on a second lightweight architecture to test generalisability.
