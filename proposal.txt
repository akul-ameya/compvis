Project Title –
The GenAI Augmentation Paradox Under Compute Constraints: Evaluating Synthetic Data in Low-Data Regimes

Description –
This project investigates whether synthetic data generated by diffusion models can recover performance lost in low-data regimes, and whether such recovery reflects meaningful representation improvement. Instead of full-scale ImageNet training, the study will use Tiny ImageNet to enable controlled experimentation under realistic compute constraints.
Three training pipelines will be constructed:
1.	Baseline: 5% real data + classical augmentations
2.	Diffusion-Boosted: 5% real data + class-conditional synthetic samples
3.	Ceiling: 100% Tiny ImageNet real data

Challenge –
The core challenge is distinguishing true information gain from simple distribution smoothing. Synthetic augmentation increases sample count, but it must be determined whether this produces improved generalisation, better calibration, and altered feature structure rather than superficial accuracy gains.
The project must also operate within strict compute constraints (single GPU or limited Colab sessions), requiring efficient model selection, controlled dataset scaling, and careful experimental design.

Dataset –
Tiny ImageNet
•	5% stratified subset for low-data regime
•	Full dataset used as performance ceiling
•	Synthetic images generated using pretrained diffusion models and cached offline
Experimental Design –
Model –
•	ResNet18 or MobileNetV2 (pretrained backbone)
•	Mixed-precision training
•	30–50 epochs with early stopping
Synthetic Data Strategy –
•	Pretrained diffusion model (no GAN training)
•	200–500 synthetic images per class (generated once and cached)
•	Synthetic samples mixed with real samples during training

Evaluation Axes –
1.	Performance Recovery –
i.	Top-1 accuracy
ii.	Gap to full-data ceiling
2.	Calibration –
i.	Expected Calibration Error (ECE)
ii.	Confidence–accuracy alignment curves
3.	Robustness –
i.	Small corruption benchmark (noise, blur, brightness shift)
ii.	Rare-class sensitivity
4.	Representation Structure (Lightweight Geometry Analysis) –
i.	Feature covariance spectral decay
ii.	Linear probe separability comparison
iii.	CKA similarity between baseline and diffusion-trained models

Justification of Choice –
This project directly addresses a central question in modern computer vision: whether generative models provide real information gain in data-scarce regimes. Rather than attempting large-scale ImageNet replication, the scaled design preserves the conceptual integrity of the original idea while ensuring feasibility within limited compute resources.
The proposal goes beyond simple accuracy benchmarking by incorporating calibration, robustness, and controlled representation analysis. This strengthens the research framing while keeping scope disciplined and executable within 3–4 weeks.
The design balances ambition and practicality:
•	It maintains a clear experimental core.
•	It incorporates analytical depth without requiring large-scale infrastructure.
•	It avoids training generative models from scratch.
•	It produces interpretable and defensible conclusions.
The project demonstrates rigorous experimental methodology, controlled ablations, and research-oriented analysis while remaining feasible under realistic computational constraints.
