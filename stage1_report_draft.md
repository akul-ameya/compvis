## I. Problem Definition (≤250 words)

We study image classification in a low-data regime on Tiny ImageNet (200 classes, 64×64 images) under realistic compute constraints. Our central question is whether synthetic images generated by large pretrained diffusion models can recover performance lost when only 5% of the real training data is available, and whether any apparent recovery reflects genuine representation improvement rather than trivial data smoothing.
Formally, the input is a color image \(x \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times 64\times 64}\) and the output is a class label \(y \in \{1,\dots,200\}\). The low-data setting (5% of Tiny ImageNet, i.e., 25 images per class) makes the problem challenging due to overfitting, miscalibration, and brittleness to small corruptions. We introduce three pipelines: (1) a **Baseline** trained on 5% real images with classical augmentations, (2) a **DiffusionBoost** model that mixes real images with class-conditional synthetic samples from Stable Diffusion, and (3) a **Ceiling** model trained on the full Tiny ImageNet training set.
Stage 1 focuses on building a robust data pipeline, training a pretrained ResNet-18 backbone, and producing initial accuracy, calibration, robustness, and feature-geometry diagnostics. In Stage 2, we will extend this baseline to diffusion-generated augmentation and investigate how synthetic data affects calibration, robustness, and representation geometry across classes.

## II. Mini Literature Survey (≤500 words)

- **Diffusion models for image synthesis.** Ho et al. (2020) formalised denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPM) as a powerful class of generative models that can produce high-fidelity images. Dhariwal and Nichol (2021) showed that diffusion models can match or outperform GANs on ImageNet-scale generation, especially when combined with classifier guidance. Rombach et al. (2022) introduced latent diffusion, enabling efficient high-resolution synthesis from text prompts under constrained compute.
- **Synthetic data augmentation.** Shorten and Khoshgoftaar (2019) surveyed classical augmentation techniques (cropping, flipping, color jittering, geometric transforms) and demonstrated their effectiveness in improving generalisation in deep networks. More recent work investigates using generative models to synthesise additional training data in low-data regimes; these studies raise questions about when synthetic data offers true information gain versus simple regularisation.
- **Tiny ImageNet and low-data regimes.** Tiny ImageNet is a widely used proxy for ImageNet-scale experiments under limited compute. Prior work uses it to benchmark architectures and augmentation strategies, often reporting Top-1 accuracy under constrained training budgets. We adopt Tiny ImageNet because it preserves the multi-class, fine-grained structure of ImageNet while remaining tractable on a single GPU.
- **Calibration of deep networks.** Guo et al. (2017) showed that modern deep classifiers are often miscalibrated, being over-confident on both clean and shifted data. They proposed temperature scaling as a simple post-hoc calibration method and popularised Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and reliability diagrams as standard metrics. Our project adopts ECE to quantify how low-data training and synthetic augmentation affect confidence alignment.
- **Representation similarity and geometry.** Kornblith et al. (2019) introduced Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) as a robust measure of representational similarity between neural networks. Complementary work examines the eigenvalue spectrum of feature covariances and linear probe performance to understand how feature geometry changes across training regimes. We use these to measure whether synthetic data shifts representations toward the full-data model.

In Stage 1, we implement the baseline pipeline and gather initial measurements from these axes (accuracy, calibration, robustness, representation structure) on the 5% Tiny ImageNet subset. In Stage 2, we will extend the same analyses to diffusion-augmented models and the full-data ceiling, positioning our results within the literature above.

## III. Exploratory Data Analysis (≤250 words)

We use Tiny ImageNet as a controlled yet realistic benchmark. The full dataset contains 200 classes with 500 training images and 50 validation images per class. To simulate a low-data regime, we construct a deterministic 5% stratified subset of the training split: for each class, we randomly sample 25 images (seeded), yielding exactly 5,000 training samples.
Our EDA confirms that the resulting subset is perfectly balanced by design (25 images per class), eliminating class imbalance as a confound. We visualise a grid of sample images spanning multiple classes (Figure 1) to qualitatively inspect diversity and label quality. All images are originally 64×64 pixels; a simple size histogram verifies the uniform resolution. We also compute channel-wise means and standard deviations and find them to be broadly consistent with standard ImageNet statistics, justifying the use of ImageNet normalisation.
For Stage 1, we resize inputs to 224×224 and apply standard augmentations (random crops, horizontal flips).

## IV. Baseline and Proof of Concept (≤250 words)

Our baseline model is a ResNet-18 pretrained on ImageNet. We replace the final fully connected layer with a 200-way classifier to match Tiny ImageNet classes and fine-tune the model on the 5% subset using AdamW (learning rate 3×10⁻⁴, batch size 64) for 5 epochs. All experiments use mixed-precision training on a single RTX 4060 GPU.
Figure 2 shows the training and validation loss/accuracy curves over epochs. The model achieves **37.15%** Top-1 accuracy on the Tiny ImageNet validation set after 5 epochs, which satisfies the Stage 1 requirement of obtaining a non-trivial baseline. Sample predictions on held-out images confirm the pipeline runs end-to-end.
Beyond accuracy, we compute Expected Calibration Error (ECE) on the validation set and obtain **ECE = 0.0398**, with the reliability diagram in Figure 3 indicating the model is mildly over-confident in this low-data setting. We also perform a corruption benchmark (Gaussian noise, blur, brightness shift) and observe drops from **37.15%** on clean data to **10.17%**, **36.72%**, and **31.15%** under perturbations, quantifying robustness. Finally, we extract penultimate-layer features, compute the covariance eigenvalue spectrum, and train a linear probe, obtaining **42.00%** accuracy — providing an initial snapshot of representation geometry for later comparison against diffusion-augmented models.

In Stage 2, we will introduce class-conditional synthetic images generated using Stable Diffusion. Beyond overall accuracy, we will investigate calibration behaviour, robustness to corruption, and representation geometry using feature covariance spectra and CKA similarity. We will also explore whether the utility of synthetic augmentation varies across classes.
